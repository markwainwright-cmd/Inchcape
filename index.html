Five key audiences — who they are and what they want
Channel: Earnings calls
Institutional analysts & fund managers
Peel Hunt, JPMorgan, BNP Paribas Exane, Berenberg, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, Citi, Liberum, Barclays, Bank of America, UBS. Proxy for the institutional shareholder base. They read across every word of every result and look for inconsistency.
Recurring question clusters — verbatim themes
  • → Chile and Colombia market recovery — raised in every call since H1 2023
  • → Capital allocation — buybacks vs M&A, leverage trajectory, when does deleveraging stop being the priority
  • → Chinese OEM exposure — opportunity framing in 2023, risk/disintermediation framing by 2026
  • → Margin normalisation — Europe, APAC, Americas through the cycle
  • → Contract wins — when do they translate to revenue, what's the maturity curve, are some OEMs going direct
  • → Free cash flow conversion — quality of working capital inflows, sustainability
  • → VLS / aftersales — when does it become a meaningful profit contribution
Representative verbatim questions
  • "
    Are you effectively walking away from the £50m profit target for VLS at this stage?
    Andy Grobler, BNP Paribas Exane — FY2023
  • "
    In a year of perhaps where you have underperformed in terms of free cash generation, are you willing to borrow to fund share buybacks?
    Andrew Nussey, Peel Hunt — FY2024
  • "
    Why don't those APAC competitive dynamics play out as Chinese OEMs continue to develop further in your other locations?
    Tim Ramskill, Bank of America — FY2025
Strategic signal for comms
This audience is not served by LinkedIn content — they consume RNS, results calls and investor presentations. But the gaps they probe (capital-light thesis, cash conversion, OEM disintermediation risk) are precisely what LinkedIn fails to argue. Closing that gap in editorial content would also strengthen the IR narrative.
Channel: LinkedIn comments
OEM partners & automotive industry
Named OEM contacts, industry operators and automotive professionals who engage publicly. These are the relationships Inchcape's entire model depends on — and several speak up unprompted on LinkedIn.
Who is engaging — named individuals
  • → Thomas Tonger (Head of Sales Europe, Daimler Buses) — welcomed Inchcape as Iceland distributor publicly on LinkedIn. The only OEM partner to validate the relationship in writing.
  • → María Cristina Torquatti (Mercedes-Benz Argentina, LATAM) — commented on the Athens Leadership Conference post
  • → ILIAS KOSMAS (COO, OPAP Group, former Inchcaper 20+ years) — "Inchcape is now a world class conglomerate. I have nothing but respect and admiration."
  • → Dario Nocioni (Digital Commerce executive) — commented substantively on DAP/DXP platforms and their role in NEV transition
What they respond to — content triggers
  • → M&A announcements — Iceland acquisition drew the highest-quality OEM-adjacent engagement
  • → Distribution partnership proof points — OEM-facing content draws industry peers
  • → Market-specific performance — Greece SME post drew a COO-level comment about Inchcape's market history there
Strategic signal for comms
OEM voices validating Inchcape publicly are rare and extremely valuable. Thomas Tonger's comment on the Iceland post is the kind of third-party endorsement that no press release can manufacture. Content that invites OEM voices — co-authored posts, named quotes, milestone celebrations — should be a priority. One OEM CEO comment is worth 50 generic congratulations.

One of the most active commenter groups — spanning senior leaders (Philip Jenkins CEO APAC, Liz Brown CSO), middle management, market-level operators, and former employees including at least one with a significant grievance. The internal audience is not a passive one.

⚠ Critical flag — disaffected former employee
  • → Mauricio Ariza Murillo (former Inchcape Colombia) — commented on five separate posts with detailed allegations of harassment, abuse of power and intimidation in the Trucks & Vans after-sales team in Colombia. The internal audience reads, shares and comments — and their voice either amplifies or undermines the brand narrative. The Ariza Murillo situation is a sustained reputational pattern that the comms team should be monitoring. People and culture content (D&I, mental health, colleague) draws disproportionate internal engagement — both positive and negative.

  • → Stefan Bomhard (Retired CEO, Imperial Brands) — congratulated on Iceland acquisition. Former Inchcape board-era connection.

What triggers their engagement
  • → M&A and major strategic announcements — Iceland acquisition drew the broadest cross-sector response
  • → Financial results — H1 2025 results video drew Unipart CEO comment
  • → Executive milestone content — leadership conference, CEO market visits
Strategic signal for comms
This audience engages around moments of strategic significance — not routine content. They are a credibility signal: when a peer CEO or former executive comments, it amplifies the post well beyond Inchcape's own follower base. Posts that attract this audience should be identified and boosted. The investor narrative gap means this audience never sees the financial model case made clearly.
Channel: LinkedIn comments
Critics, market observers & noise
A mixed category — ranging from substantive industry critics providing genuine pushback, to negative market observers (particularly from Chile and Colombia post-Derco), to irrelevant spam. Each requires a different response posture.
Substantive critics — worth engaging
  • → IBTISSAM ABOUL (Distribution Network Controller, Auto Hall) — detailed technical critique of Inchcape's NEV adoption post, challenging the demand-side framing and raising supply chain and battery sovereignty issues. A genuine industry expert disagreeing thoughtfully.
  • → Ingrid Vivian B. (Global Logistics professional) — constructive response to the Drivers of Change post, affirming local market dynamics point
Market-level dissenters — Latin America
  • → Matias Vasquez Osores (Chilean market observer) — commented in strong terms on two Derco-related posts calling the acquisition "the worst purchase they ever made" (in caps). Likely a former dealer, franchisee or market participant.
Noise — spam and irrelevant
  • → PR agency solicitation (Zhanna Solonenko / OBA PR) posted a sales pitch on the Iceland announcement
  • → Fiverr digital marketing promotion on the NEV post
  • → Recurring emoji-only commenter (LY Lim) on multiple posts — affirmative but content-free
Strategic signal for comms
The substantive critics are an asset if engaged — IBTISSAM ABOUL's comment on NEV supply chain is precisely the kind of expert pushback that, if responded to, demonstrates thought leadership rather than defensiveness. The Chilean/Derco criticism reflects a real market sentiment that surfaces in analyst questions too. Treat these as listening posts, not threats.

Where the two channels diverge
The same topics surface very differently in analyst Q&A and LinkedIn comments — revealing misalignments between what Inchcape says and what its audiences actually want to understand.
Topic Analysts ask What institutional audiences probe LinkedIn says What public commenters respond to The gap
Chile & LatAm Market recovery timeline, TIV trajectory, margin through the cycle, synergy completion — asked at every call since H1 2023 with granular data requests Chilean commenters call the Derco acquisition "the worst purchase they ever made" (in capitals). Former employees from Colombia describe hostile management. Narrative disconnect is total. The local market reality and the investor thesis are not in dialogue with each other
Capital model Capital-light thesis, free cash flow conversion quality, working capital sustainability, when buybacks become structural vs opportunistic — the most technically demanding line of questioning Almost no engagement with the financial model. Six posts in 68 touch it at all. Commenters engage on people, milestones and ESG — never on capital returns. Inchcape's most distinctive investment attribute is invisible to its LinkedIn audience
Chinese OEMs Framed as disintermediation risk by 2025–26: "Why won't what happened with BYD in Belgium happen elsewhere?" Analysts want a structural defence, not optimism. LinkedIn commenters engage positively with Chinese brand announcements. The BYD/Belux situation barely registers in public comments. Risk is live in IR but invisible in editorial. A strategic post on Inchcape's OEM portfolio philosophy would serve both audiences
People & culture Rarely asked — only surfaces in remuneration and incentive scheme questions (EPS alignment, LTIP weighting). Analysts don't probe culture; they probe whether management incentives are aligned. Highest volume of LinkedIn engagement. But also the channel through which the most damaging reputation content arrives — Ariza Murillo's repeated allegations of harassment at Inchcape Colombia. Culture is Inchcape's highest-engagement content theme and its highest reputational vulnerability simultaneously
OEM partnerships Probed forensically — contract maturity curves, revenue contribution timing, minimum viable returns, what happens when OEMs go direct. Analysts want the commercial logic, not the relationship story. Thomas Tonger (Daimler Buses) and María Cristina Torquatti (Mercedes-Benz) both comment publicly — the most valuable endorsements in the dataset. But they are not invited or cultivated. OEM partner voices are available and willing. They are not being used strategically.
NEV transition Market-specific: COE cycle in Singapore, BEV penetration dynamics in APAC, multi-powertrain approach, which markets are structurally advantaged. Granular and regional. The Drivers of Change report generates genuine industry debate — including the most substantive critical comment in the dataset (IBTISSAM ABOUL on supply chain). This audience wants to be argued with. LinkedIn has a ready audience for a sharper, more argued NEV point of view — the Drivers of Change asset is under-exploited
